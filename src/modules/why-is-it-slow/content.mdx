> You asked your agent for a page showing your 50 contacts, each with their
> latest message. It built it. It works. It also takes six seconds to load,
> and you can feel every one of them.
>
> The code looks tiny — a loop, a few lines. Your laptop plays 4K video
> without breaking a sweat. How is a list of fifty names *slow*?

Here's a way to feel the answer instead of memorizing it. Computers do
different kinds of work at wildly different prices, and the prices are hard
to picture at real speed. So stretch time: pretend one processor operation
takes **one second** — a snap of your fingers. On that clock, how expensive
is everything else?

>

<Ladder />

That last rung is the one to sit with. A **round trip** — your request
travels to a machine somewhere else, and the answer travels back — costs
*years* on a clock where ordinary work costs seconds. Nothing else on the
ladder is even in the same neighborhood.

</Predict>

So the question "why is it slow?" almost never means "is the computer doing
a lot of work?" It means: **how many trips is it making?**

Back to your contacts page. There are two honest ways an agent might build
it. Plan A makes one trip, brings everything home, and does the rest in
memory. Plan B makes one trip for the list — then goes back across the
internet once *per contact*, because that's the most natural-looking loop in
the world to write.

<Predict
  id="why-is-it-slow.race-winner"
  kind="choice"
  prompt="Plan A does a million operations in memory. Plan B makes 51 small network calls. Which loads first?"
  options={['A by a lot (10× or more)', 'roughly even', 'B by a lot (10× or more)']}
  actual="A by a lot (10× or more)"
  judge={(g, a) => g === a}
>

Run it and watch. Both plans execute for real, on the same price list as the
ladder — nothing here is animated to a script.

<Race />

Try the two twists. Drag Plan A's in-memory work up to ten million
operations and watch its bar barely move — work in memory is nearly free at
this scale. Then flip on humanized time and reread the same race: a million
operations cost about *12 days*, and every single little network call costs
about *3 years*.

One more word, now that you've watched the calls arrive: no two of them
took the same time. That spread — the waiting time of each individual call,
some quick, some slow — is called **latency**, and its slow tail is why
"usually fast" pages still feel bad.

And the shape you just watched lose the race has a name too: **the N+1
pattern** — one call for the list, then one more call per item, N+1 trips in
all. It's one of the most common things agents build by accident, because
the loop reads so naturally. Now you can see it: the number of trips grows
with your data.

</Predict>

## When this doesn't matter

Honesty about the other direction: at 50 contacts, even Plan B is
*survivable* — sluggish, not broken. A small app with a handful of users
does not need caching layers or clever infrastructure; it needs the right
number of trips. And yes, calls can be made in parallel instead of one after
another — that helps, has limits of its own, and gets its own lesson later.
The question to carry out of this one is not "is this architecture fancy
enough?" but "how many trips, and does that number grow?"
